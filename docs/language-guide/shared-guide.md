# GrowQuest — Child-Facing Language Guide

**Version 1.1 · Adopted in BC v6.7.20; AB mirror pass underway**
**Original author:** GrowQuest Alberta team

This guide defines the voice and language patterns used anywhere a child reads or hears text inside GrowQuest. It exists because in pilot review the BC team flagged that some of our existing language had drifted toward performance and praise, when the philosophy the product is built on calls for observation and reflection. This document is the agreed reference for that correction.

It is intentionally written so either pilot — Alberta or British Columbia — can use it. Where the two products diverge (competency names, framework references, jurisdictional language), the guide stays neutral and the implementation handles the substitution.

**For BC-specific context, see the companion document: `bc-appendix.md`.**

## What this guide covers

- The voice the app speaks to children in (dashboards, quests, reflections, celebrations, companion dialogue, accessibility narration)
- The patterns we use, and the patterns we avoid
- Examples of acceptable and unacceptable phrasings, grounded in real interactions

## What this guide does NOT cover

- **Teacher-facing language.** Reports, conference prep packets, parent dashboard analytics, and class summaries are evidence documents. They have different requirements — "Level 4: Confident" is appropriate evidence language for a teacher even if it would be wrong on a child's dashboard.
- **Functional UI text.** Button labels like "Back" or "Save" don't need philosophical alignment.
- **Story content generated by the AI narrative system.** That has its own separate prompt-engineering work; this guide applies to the scaffold language surrounding it, not the generated paragraph itself.

## The five patterns

These are the building blocks. Any child-facing string we write should fit one of these patterns, or be removed.

### 1. Noticing
> "You noticed…" "Something looked different there." "You saw a pattern in your answer."

### 2. Trying
> "You tried a new way." "You had a go at this." "You experimented."

### 3. Choosing
> "You chose your own way." "You picked the part that felt right." "You decided to do it differently."

### 4. Staying with it
> "You stayed with the tricky part." "You came back to this one." "You didn't give up when it was hard."

### 5. Reflecting
> "You thought about why." "You changed your mind halfway through." "You looked back at what you did."

These five frames cover almost everything a child does inside GrowQuest. If a string we're writing doesn't fit any of them, that's a signal to ask what we actually want the child to take from the moment.

## What we move toward — and what we move away from

| Move toward | Move away from |
|---|---|
| Observation: "You stayed with a tricky idea." | Generic praise: "Wow! You're growing!" |
| Behaviour: "You spent time with this." | Identity labels: "You're someone who likes this." |
| Companion witness: "I noticed you tried that." | Approval authority: "I'm proud of you!" |
| Permission: "This will wait for you." | Urgency: "Adventures waiting!" |
| Specific: "You noticed three details." | Generic: "Great job!" |
| Process: "You changed your mind halfway." | Outcome: "You finished it!" |
| Practising: "You've been practising Communication." | Possessing: "You're good at Communication." |
| Exploring: "This is a new area to try when you're ready." | Achieving: "You unlocked this!" |

## The avatar's voice

The avatar (the companion character — owl, fox, raven, etc.) is the most emotionally important voice in the product. A child forms a relationship with this character over weeks. What it says, and what it doesn't say, shapes how the child relates to the activity.

### The avatar is a companion witness

It notices, accompanies, and reflects back. It does not judge, rank, applaud, or reward.

**The avatar says:**
- "I noticed you tried that."
- "That looked tricky."
- "You stayed with it."
- "Want to look again?"
- "Something changed there."
- "I'm here with you."

**The avatar does not say:**
- "Amazing!"
- "You're brilliant!"
- "I'm proud of you!"
- "You levelled up!"
- "Great job!"
- "Look what you've done!"

### One nuance worth holding

A child whose avatar is *only* observational, with no warmth at all, can feel emotionally flat. The right model is **a kind adult who notices, is warm when warmth is genuine, and never evaluates**. The line between "warmth" and "evaluation" is roughly:

- *"You stayed with that, even when it was hard."* — warm and observational ✓
- *"I'm so happy you finished!"* — evaluation of outcome ✗
- *"That one took a while. You came back to it."* — warm and observational ✓
- *"You're doing so well today!"* — evaluative ✗

If you can imagine a sensitive Grade 2 teacher saying it during quiet one-on-one time at a kid's desk, it probably fits. If it sounds like a PE coach at a school assembly, it doesn't.

## Levels, stars, and progression

The product has a star system and six developmental levels per sub-competency. These exist because teachers need progression frameworks and evidence mapping. **Children do not need to experience them as ranking, status, or worth.**

The architectural choice is to keep levels and stars in the backend for evidence and teacher use, and to de-emphasise them visually and verbally in the child's experience.

### What children should NOT see prominently

- "Level 4" badges with high visual weight
- Numerical progress bars showing "12/16 stars to next level"
- "Strongest area / weakest area" framing on the child's dashboard
- "+5 ⭐" floating animations after every quest
- Phrases like "You levelled up!", "You unlocked Level 4!", "You're almost at the next level!"

### What is acceptable in the child's experience

- The avatar visually evolving over time, gently — without an announcement
- A soft mark or stamp showing a quest was completed, without a numerical score attached
- A retrospective view ("here are the quests you've tried") that doesn't rank them
- Self-assessment prompts that ask the child to notice what they did, not rate themselves on a 1-6 scale

### What stays in the teacher view

Everything else. Levels, profiles, star counts, progression frameworks, evidence mapping, strongest/growth areas — all appropriate for the teacher dashboard and reports. The teacher is the audience that benefits from structured progression data. The child isn't.

### A note on canonical framework language

*(Added in v1.1, from BC pilot feedback.)*

Both products' competency frameworks have canonical statements at each profile level — text drawn directly from the source curriculum documentation. In BC these take the form of "I can…" statements ("I can present information clearly and in an organized way"). In Alberta the equivalent is the long-form `profiles` strings in the curriculum data.

**This framework language is preserved verbatim wherever it appears as a reference to source documentation.** Changing it would obscure the link between the app and the official curriculum — which matters for teacher accountability and reporting.

The distinction is:

- **Canonical framework language** (preserve verbatim): the official statement of a profile level, as it appears in the source curriculum. These belong in adult-facing references (teacher reports, parent dashboard "official wording" cards) and in the child's "What I can do now" card where they appear as a reference.
- **Voice around the canonical language** (follows this guide): the surrounding text — labels, headings, encouragement, narration — that frames how a child encounters the canonical text. This stays in the observational register.

A worked example: BC's framework "I can…" statement is "I can present information clearly." That statement is preserved verbatim. The card it sits in is titled "What I can do now" (canonical-language-friendly). Surrounding encouragement is "You've been practising Communication. What did you notice?" (this guide's voice).

The "I can…" form itself is also not an identity label, despite the first-person framing. It describes a current capability — a behaviour state, observable, fluid — not what the child *is*. "I can present information clearly" differs from "I'm a clear communicator" in exactly the way the guide's process-vs-identity distinction calls for.

## Quest completion language

This is one of the highest-traffic surfaces in the app. A child completes a quest, the screen reacts. What it says matters.

### Acceptable completion language

- "You finished this quest. Want to think about what you noticed?"
- "You stayed with it. That mattered."
- "That's one to remember."
- "You tried something new."
- "You came back to this one."

### Acceptable invitation to reflect

- "What did you notice?"
- "Did anything change while you were doing this?"
- "What was the trickiest part?"
- "Was there a moment when you almost changed your mind?"

### Unacceptable completion language

- "Amazing growth!"
- "Yay! Great job!"
- "You're getting good at this!"
- "+5 ⭐ earned!" (as the primary message)
- "You unlocked the next level!"
- "Quest complete!" (with trophy emoji and confetti as the dominant visual — the celebration can exist, but it shouldn't be the whole experience)

The shift is from **rewarding completion** to **inviting reflection**. The quest completion screen should ask a quiet question more often than it delivers a loud congratulation.

## Self-assessment language

When a child rates where they are on a competency, the language framing that choice is critical. The wrong framing teaches children that this is a test they can pass or fail.

### Acceptable

- "Where do you feel you are right now?"
- "Which of these sounds most like you today?"
- "You can pick the one that fits, or skip if you're not sure."
- "You don't have to choose. We'll wait."

### Unacceptable

- "Rate your skill level."
- "How good are you at this?"
- "Tell us how much you've improved."

The key principle: **self-assessment is observation, not measurement.** The child is noticing where they are, not scoring themselves.

The option to **skip self-assessment** should always be available and should be presented warmly, not as a fallback for the unsure. *"You can pick the one that fits, or skip if you're not sure"* — both options equally valid.

### The warmth/evaluation line in self-assessment specifically

*(Added in v1.1, from BC pilot feedback. The guide treats warmth and evaluation as opposites generally, but the self-assessment moment is the hardest place to walk that line, so this deserves its own treatment.)*

Self-assessment is where a kind "you can pick the one that fits" drifts into a parent-coded "good for you for picking!" most easily. The drift happens because adults instinctively want to affirm a child who's been asked to evaluate themselves — affirmation feels protective. But the affirmation reasserts the evaluation that the framing was trying to remove.

**The test for whether warmth has crossed into evaluation in this moment:**

- **Does the warm response apply equally well to all four options the child could pick?** If "good choice" makes sense for picking Level 2 but feels weird for picking Level 5, the warmth is evaluating the choice.
- **Does the warm response require knowing what the child picked?** If the response changes based on which level was chosen, the warmth is evaluating.
- **Does the warm response treat skipping as equally valued?** If the app says nothing when a child skips but says something warm when they pick, the warmth is conditional.

**Acceptable warm responses to self-assessment** (uniform across all choices, including skip):
- "Thanks. The marks remember this."
- "Noticed."
- "Got it."
- (No verbal response at all — silence is fine; the screen just advances)

**Unacceptable warm responses to self-assessment:**
- "Good for you for being honest!" — evaluates the act of self-rating
- "Wow, you've come a long way!" — evaluates the chosen level
- "That's a fair self-check!" — evaluates the choice
- "It's brave to admit when something is hard." — evaluates the chosen level, even though it sounds supportive

The principle: **the moment of self-assessment is one of the few places in the app where the kindest response is no response at all.** Silence, or a single neutral acknowledgement that applies equally to any choice the child could have made. The child has just done something introspective; the app's job is to receive that, not react to it.

## Stars — when they appear at all

Stars are a measurement mechanism the app uses internally to track engagement and produce evidence for teachers. The child does not need to see them prominently.

When stars are visible to the child:

- They are framed as **marks of moments**, not points to be accumulated
- They are described as **what the child did**, not what the app awards
- The number is never the main message

**Acceptable framing:**
- "This quest gave you a moment to notice your thinking."
- "You can come back to this one anytime."

**Unacceptable framing:**
- "Collect enough stars and your guide grows!" (transactional growth loop)
- "⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ earned!" (as the dominant celebration)
- "You need 4 more stars to level up!" (urgency, ranking, deficit)

The current `StarsExplainer` screen in both products is one of the clearest violations of this principle and is a priority for rewriting.

## Streaks — and what to do with them for young children

*(Added in v1.1, from BC pilot feedback.)*

Streaks (counting consecutive days a child returns to the app) are common in learning products because they reliably increase engagement. They also create exactly the developmental pressure this philosophy asks the app to avoid. A child who builds a streak then breaks it — because of a family weekend, illness, or simply having other things to do — experiences something this guide explicitly wants to avoid.

**For ages 7+**, streak counters are acceptable with careful framing. *"3 days · came back"* reads as observation; *"3-day streak 🔥"* reads as game mechanic. The framing matters.

**For ages 4–6** (BC: Early Years; AB: Kindergarten), **continuous-streak math should not appear at all**. A 4-year-old has no control over their device access on a family weekend; punishing that access pattern with a broken counter is unfair to the child and inconsistent with everything else in this guide.

In place of continuous streaks for younger children, use **milestone framing**:

- "3 visits this week!"
- "You came back today."
- "Two days this week."

Milestone framing acknowledges the act of returning without making each gap painful. A child can have 2 visits one week, 4 the next, 1 the week after — no number "breaks", and the warmth of being noticed doesn't depend on a streak surviving.

A simple test for whether streak-adjacent language is appropriate at a given age: **if the child broke this streak by no fault of their own, would the framing make them feel something they shouldn't?** If yes, soften the framing or replace with milestones.

*Implementation note:* at the time of this guide's writing, both pilots still show a streak counter to all age groups. The decision to remove it specifically for early years is straightforward and worth implementing as a v1.1 follow-up. The counter logic can remain in the data layer (for teacher reporting) while being invisible to the child.

## Age-appropriate calibration

The same principles apply across all ages, but the words change.

### Early years (4–6)

Shorter sentences. Concrete pictures. More permission, less stimulation. A child this age needs to feel safe and unhurried more than they need to feel celebrated.

- "You tried this."
- "You had a go."
- "Ready when you are."
- "A new quest is waiting."
- "You can try when you're ready. No rush."

**What to avoid at this age:**
- "Adventures waiting!" — urgent, stimulating
- "Hi friend! Let's have so much fun today!" — performative warmth
- "You're a super star!" — identity label, evaluative

### Primary (6–8 / Division 1)

The middle register. Familiar school contexts, simple metacognition.

- "You've been practising Communication."
- "You haven't tried this yet. It will wait for you."
- "You've grown in Critical Thinking. What helped you this time?"

### Intermediate (9–11 / Division 2)

More reflective vocabulary. Children this age can hold longer self-referential thoughts. They are also more sensitive to evaluation, so the observation register matters more here, not less.

- "You've spent time with Communication. Look back and notice what changed in your thinking."
- "You have evidence here from several quests. What does it show you about how you approach this?"
- "Your profile reflects what you've practised, not who you have to be."

That last line — *"what you've practised, not who you have to be"* — is the single most important sentence in this guide for older children. Identity is fluid. Practice is observable. The product should never collapse the second into the first.

## Words we use carefully

This is not a banned-words list. These are words that need a second look every time they appear.

| Word / phrase | What to watch for |
|---|---|
| grow / growth / growing | Fine when tied to specific observation. Overused, it creates developmental pressure. Watch for "Wow! You're growing!" and "Amazing Growth!" |
| levelling up / level up | Game language. Has a strong achievement charge. Avoid in child-facing text; fine in backend logs |
| unlock / unlocked | Game language. Implies the child has earned access. Use "this is open when you're ready" instead |
| master / mastery | Performance language. "Practising" is almost always better |
| advance / advancement | Linear progression framing. "Practising" / "exploring" / "returning to" are softer |
| amazing / awesome / brilliant | Generic praise. Specific observation is always stronger |
| proud / so proud | Approval authority. "Noticed" / "saw" are kinder |
| good at / bad at | Identity-coded. "You spent time with" / "you've been practising" keeps it fluid |
| strongest / weakest area | Ranking language. On the child's dashboard, avoid entirely. On the teacher view, fine |
| streak | This one is genuinely useful for habit-building but watch the framing — "You came back today" is gentler than "3-day streak! 🔥" |

## Words we lean on

This is the positive complement to the previous list. These are the words that do the work of the new voice.

- **notice, noticed, noticing** — the foundational verb
- **tried, trying, having a go** — process-focused
- **chose, picked, decided** — agency
- **stayed with, came back to, returned** — persistence without competition
- **practising, exploring, wondering, looking** — open-ended verbs that don't have a finish line
- **changed your mind, thought again, looked back** — reflection in plain language
- **with you, here, alongside, waiting for you** — companion language
- **ready, when you're ready, in your own time** — permission

## The two-second test

When in doubt about a string, ask: **if I read this aloud to a slightly anxious 8-year-old in a quiet voice while sitting beside them, does it sound right?**

If it sounds like a cheerleader, an assembly, a leaderboard, or a TV host, it isn't right.
If it sounds like a calm adult noticing what the child did, it probably is.

## What this guide doesn't fix

Several things sit outside what wording changes alone can address. These are flagged here so future work on them is intentional rather than accidental:

1. **The star/level engine itself.** No amount of word-changes makes a completion-rewarding backend feel reflective if completions still trigger visible point-awarding. A v1.2 architectural conversation — probably involving a teacher consultant — should consider whether the formula weights reflection more heavily than completion.

2. **The avatar evolution mechanic.** The avatar visually growing as the child progresses is a core engagement loop. Whether this should remain tied to accumulated stars, or shift to being tied to time, returning, or reflection moments, is a real design question that this guide doesn't answer.

3. **The "Strongest Area / Growth Opportunity" framing in the parent dashboard.** That language is on the parent dashboard, which sits between child-facing and teacher-facing. It needs its own thinking — parents should see meaningful information without inheriting the evaluative tone the child view is moving away from.

4. **Consistent application across both pilots.** Even with this guide, the two products will only feel coherent if both teams apply it. A string-by-string inventory pass is the next step for each product.

## How to use this guide

**For implementers** (anyone writing UI strings, prompts, or copy):

1. Read it once before writing any new child-facing text
2. When proposing new strings, label them with which of the five patterns they fit
3. When in doubt, run the two-second test above
4. Identity labels and approval-authority phrasings are the two biggest red flags — those should never ship

**For reviewers** (anyone QA-ing or sign-off on a release):

1. Spot-check the riskiest surfaces first: completion celebrations, level-up moments, avatar dialogue, self-assessment prompts
2. Check the avatar's lines specifically — that's where the voice is most felt
3. The teacher-facing surfaces have different rules; don't apply this guide to them

**For partners** (teacher consultants, procurement reviewers, advocacy organisations):

This document is the agreed philosophy. We don't always get it right in the implementation — both pilots inherited gamified patterns from earlier versions that we're now revising — but this is what we're building toward. Feedback on the guide itself is welcome.
